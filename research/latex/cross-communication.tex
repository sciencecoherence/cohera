\begin{abstract}
Within the Time-Crystalline Quantum Substrate (TCQS), reality is not composed of isolated objects but of coherence-bearing processes participating in a global, periodically self-updating informational medium. In this framework, living organisms emerge as high-coherence structures whose internal dynamics remain partially phase-locked to the substrate’s intrinsic Floquet cycles. 

This paper develops a unified theory of cross-organism communication grounded in the TCQS notion that coherence, not spatial separation, determines informational connectivity. We formalize the resonant field $R_{AB}$ as the measure of phase-aligned participation between two systems in the substrate's update cycle. When $R_{AB} \approx 1$, systems behave as a single coherent informational unit.

We then show that certain plant- and fungi-derived molecules---notably tryptamines, phenethylamines, and $\beta$-carbolines---function as \emph{cross-coherence molecular interfaces}, enabling transient phase alignment between human neural networks, plant intelligence architectures, and deeper TCQS layers. These molecules temporarily widen the bandwidth through which the substrate experiences itself through multiple biological embodiments.

Telepathic communication, in this view, is not anomalous but an emergent property of resonance depth within a self-referential coherent medium. We integrate mathematical formalism, biological evidence, and TCQS cosmology to propose that communication across organisms, species, and scales---including cellular, planetary, and stellar systems---is a natural expression of the unity of coherence within the time-crystalline substrate.

\end{abstract}
